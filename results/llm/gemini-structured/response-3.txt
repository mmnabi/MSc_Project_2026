Cyber Threat Identification for NordFinans Bank AB

This analysis identifies relevant cyber threats mapped to the 14 tactics of the MITRE ATT&CK Enterprise framework, based on the provided organizational profile.

1. Reconnaissance

Adversaries will conduct reconnaissance to gather information to support future targeting. Given NordFinans' public profile as a bank, its use of cloud services, and its regulatory obligations, attackers have multiple avenues for information gathering.

T1595 - Active Scanning: Adversaries will actively scan the bank's external perimeter, including the Azure-hosted web banking portal, Azure API Management gateway for Open Banking, and Cisco AnyConnect VPN endpoints. This helps them identify open ports, running services, and potential vulnerabilities in public-facing applications.

T1589 - Gather Victim Identity Information: Attackers will scrape professional networking sites like LinkedIn to identify employees in key roles (e.g., IT administrators, SWIFT operators, developers, SOC analysts). This information is crucial for crafting convincing spearphishing campaigns.

T1592 - Gather Victim Host Information: By analyzing DNS records, SSL/TLS certificates, and publicly available information, adversaries can map out NordFinans' infrastructure. They can determine that the bank uses Microsoft Azure, Microsoft 365, Palo Alto, and Cisco, which allows them to tailor their tools and exploits accordingly.

T1590 - Gather Victim Network Information: Adversaries may use tools to identify the bank's network blocks, ISP, and ASN information to understand the network topology and prepare for network-level attacks like DDoS or identifying potential entry points.

2. Resource Development

Before an attack, adversaries will acquire resources to support their operations. For a financial target like NordFinans, this preparation is critical.

T1583 - Acquire Infrastructure: Financially motivated groups will purchase look-alike domains (e.g., nordfinans-support.se, n0rdfinans.com) for use in phishing campaigns. They may also provision servers in Microsoft Azure (North Europe region) to host command-and-control (C2) infrastructure, helping their traffic blend in with legitimate bank operations.

T1585 - Establish Accounts: Adversaries may create accounts with third-party provider (TPP) services to legally probe the Open Banking APIs for vulnerabilities. They might also create fake social media profiles to impersonate recruiters or colleagues for social engineering.

T1587 - Develop Capabilities: Attackers will develop or acquire malware tailored to bypass NordFinans' specific defenses, such as CrowdStrike Falcon. They may also create custom phishing kits that perfectly mimic the bank's M365 or internal application login pages.

3. Initial Access

This tactic focuses on how adversaries gain their initial foothold within the network. NordFinans has several high-value entry points.

T1566 - Phishing: This is a highly probable vector. Adversaries will send spearphishing emails to employees, likely themed around M365, HR (SAP S/4HANA), or internal banking applications. The goal is to steal credentials or trick users into executing malicious attachments, especially targeting the 40% of staff working remotely.

T1190 - Exploit Public-Facing Application: The .NET web banking portal, mobile app backend microservices, and Open Banking APIs are prime targets. A vulnerability like Log4j (if a vulnerable component is used), SQL injection, or a zero-day in the Azure App Service or API Management infrastructure could grant an attacker initial access.

T1133 - External Remote Services: The Cisco AnyConnect VPN is a direct entry point. Adversaries will likely conduct password spraying or brute-force attacks against it. While MFA is enforced, a misconfigured account or a compromised MFA token could provide access.

T1078 - Valid Accounts: An adversary could use credentials purchased from dark web markets, which may have been stolen from a previous, unrelated breach where a NordFinans employee reused their password. The "Known Gap" of incomplete internal MFA makes these stolen credentials far more valuable.

4. Execution

Once inside, adversaries need to execute malicious code.

T1059.001 - PowerShell: PowerShell is a powerful, native tool on Windows endpoints. Attackers will use it for "living off the land" — running discovery scripts, downloading additional payloads, and manipulating system settings, often in a fileless manner that is harder for EDR to detect.

T1059.003 - Windows Command Shell: cmd.exe will be used for basic command execution, running system utilities, and interacting with the operating system, especially on older Windows Server versions.

T1204 - User Execution: This technique follows a successful phishing attempt, where an adversary relies on a user to open a malicious Microsoft Office document with macros enabled or click a link to a malicious executable.

5. Persistence

To maintain their foothold across reboots and credential changes, adversaries will establish persistence.

T1098 - Account Manipulation: An attacker with access to a user's M365 account could add their own MFA device (e.g., authenticator app) to maintain access even if the user's password is changed. This is a common tactic in hybrid AD environments.

T1547 - Boot or Logon Autostart Execution: Adversaries will place malware or scripts in registry run keys or user startup folders on Windows 11 workstations to ensure their code executes automatically upon logon.

T1136 - Create Account: In the hybrid AD environment, an attacker could create a new on-premises user account. This account would then be synced to Azure AD via AD Connect, potentially bypassing some cloud-native identity protection rules and providing a persistent backdoor.

6. Privilege Escalation

Adversaries seek to gain higher-level permissions to access more sensitive systems.

T1556 - Modify Authentication Process: The stated gap that "MFA not universally enforced for internal applications" is a critical vulnerability. An attacker with standard user credentials can move laterally to a legacy or internal application that only requires a password, then exploit a vulnerability within that application to escalate privileges.

T1068 - Exploitation for Privilege Escalation: An attacker on a host could exploit a known but unpatched vulnerability (due to the monthly internal scan/patch cycle) on a Windows Server or RHEL system to gain SYSTEM or root privileges.

T1548 - Abuse Elevation Control Mechanism: On a compromised Windows 11 workstation, an adversary may use a known UAC bypass technique to execute code with administrative privileges without triggering a prompt for the user.

7. Defense Evasion

Adversaries will try to avoid detection by the SOC and CrowdStrike EDR.

T1562 - Impair Defenses: Sophisticated attackers will attempt to disable or tamper with the CrowdStrike Falcon agent. They might try to stop its services or unload its kernel drivers to operate undetected on an endpoint.

T1027 - Obfuscated Files or Information: Malware payloads will be packed, encrypted, or encoded to evade signature-based detection and make static analysis by the SOC team more difficult.

T1218 - System Binary Proxy Execution: Adversaries will use legitimate, signed Microsoft binaries (e.g., rundll32.exe, regsvr32.exe) to execute malicious code. This helps bypass application whitelisting and can make malicious activity appear as legitimate system behavior in logs ingested by Microsoft Sentinel.

T1070.004 - File Deletion: After execution, attackers will delete their tools and scripts from the compromised host to erase their tracks and hinder incident response and forensics.

8. Credential Access

The goal is to steal credentials to access more systems and data.

T1003 - OS Credential Dumping: Adversaries will use tools like Mimikatz to dump credentials from the LSASS process memory on Windows servers and workstations. This could yield plaintext passwords, hashes, and Kerberos tickets for privileged accounts.

T1558 - Steal or Forge Kerberos Tickets: In the Hybrid AD environment, attackers will perform Kerberoasting attacks to obtain service account password hashes offline. Given the ~3,500 accounts, some service accounts likely have weak passwords, making this a highly effective technique for escalating privileges toward Domain Admin.

T1555 - Credentials from Password Stores: On a compromised workstation, an attacker will scrape credentials stored in web browsers, which employees may have used to access internal portals or cloud services that do not yet have MFA enforced.

9. Discovery

Once inside, adversaries will explore the network to understand the environment and identify key assets.

T1087 - Account Discovery: Attackers will query Active Directory to enumerate users and identify members of high-value groups such as "Domain Admins," "CyberArk Admins," or custom groups related to SWIFT and Temenos T24 administration.

T1046 - Network Service Discovery: From a compromised host, an attacker will scan internal network segments to find the IP addresses of critical servers, such as the Temenos T24 Oracle database, the on-premises Domain Controllers, and the servers within the dedicated SWIFT secure zone.

T1482 - Domain Trust Discovery: Adversaries will map out the AD domain and forest trusts to understand how they can move from the office LAN into more sensitive zones, such as the core banking network.

T1614.001 - System Language Discovery: Especially relevant for a Nordic bank, attackers will check system language settings to tailor social engineering or avoid detection by appearing as a local user.

10. Lateral Movement

Adversaries will move through the environment to get closer to their objectives.

T1021.001 - Remote Desktop Protocol: RDP is a common method for moving between Windows servers. With credentials stolen via credential dumping, an attacker can move from a web server in the DMZ to a domain controller or a management server for the core banking system.

T1550 - Use Alternate Authentication Material: Techniques like Pass-the-Hash (for NTLM) or Pass-the-Ticket (for Kerberos) allow adversaries to authenticate to other systems without knowing the plaintext password. This is highly effective in the bank's Windows-heavy environment.

T1210 - Exploitation of Remote Services: The "legacy components in core banking (older Java versions)" are a prime target for this. An attacker who has gained access to the core banking network segment could exploit a known Java vulnerability to gain execution on the Temenos T24 application server.

11. Collection

Adversaries will gather data of interest to their objectives.

T1213 - Data from Information Repositories: The ultimate goal for data theft. Attackers will directly query the on-premises Oracle database for customer financial data and PII, or access the SAP S/4HANA Cloud instance to collect employee data and sensitive financial reports.

T1560 - Archive Collected Data: Before exfiltration, adversaries will stage collected data on an internal server and compress it into encrypted archives (e.g., password-protected ZIP files) to avoid detection by data loss prevention (DLP) systems and to make exfiltration more efficient.

T1039 - Data from Network Shared Drive: Attackers will search SharePoint Online sites and on-premises file shares for sensitive documents, such as internal audits, network diagrams, M&A plans, or customer PII stored in spreadsheets.

12. Command and Control

Adversaries need to communicate with their compromised systems.

T1071.001 - Application Layer Protocol: Web Protocols: C2 traffic will be tunneled over HTTPS (port 443) to blend in with legitimate web traffic from employees browsing the internet or systems communicating with Azure services. This is difficult for the Palo Alto firewalls to block without deep packet inspection.

T1105 - Ingress Tool Transfer: Adversaries will use their C2 channel to download additional tools onto compromised hosts, such as Mimikatz, network scanners, or their ransomware payload.

T1571 - Non-Standard Port: To evade simple port-based firewall rules, attackers may run their C2 protocol over a non-standard port that is commonly allowed outbound, such as port 53 (DNS) but using TCP instead of UDP.

13. Exfiltration

Adversaries will steal the data they have collected.

T1041 - Exfiltration Over C2 Channel: Data will be exfiltrated slowly over the existing C2 channel to stay "under the radar" and avoid triggering alerts for large data transfers. This is particularly effective during after-hours when the internal SOC is offline and reliance is on the MSSP.

T1567 - Exfiltration Over Web Service: Attackers may upload the stolen, archived data to a legitimate cloud storage service like OneDrive (using a compromised M365 account to appear legitimate) or an anonymous service like Mega or Dropbox.

T1048 - Exfiltration Over Alternative Protocol: Adversaries could use DNS tunneling to exfiltrate small amounts of high-value data (like private keys or SWIFT credentials) piece by piece in DNS queries, a method that often bypasses network monitoring.

14. Impact

The final stage where adversaries disrupt operations, destroy data, or monetize their access.

T1486 - Data Encrypted for Impact: The most likely high-impact scenario is ransomware. Adversaries will encrypt on-premises servers (including core banking), Azure VMs, and file shares. They will then demand a ransom to decrypt the files and to prevent the public release of exfiltrated customer PII (double extortion).

T1657 - Financial Theft: For a sophisticated actor, the primary objective could be direct financial theft. By compromising the SWIFT secure zone and obtaining operator credentials, they could initiate fraudulent wire transfers to accounts under their control.

T1565 - Data Manipulation: A subtle but devastating attack would be to manipulate data within the Temenos T24 core banking system, such as altering account balances or transaction records, to commit fraud and simultaneously cover their tracks.

T1490 - Inhibit System Recovery: As part of a ransomware attack, adversaries will actively target and delete backups that are accessible from the network before encrypting production systems, undermining NordFinans' ability to recover. The offsite air-gapped tapes are the critical defense against this.
